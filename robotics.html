Robotics Competition

My Robotics Journey: From VEX to RoboMaster

My passion for robotics ignited in middle school through VEX Robotics Competitions and evolved throughout high school and university with RoboMaster. These events are more than just contests; they're a hands-on fusion of sport, strategy, and engineering, where students worldwide design, build, and program robots to solve challenges and compete at the highest levels. From strategic game-based competitions to tactical robotics battles, each experience has deepened my expertise in mechanical design, control systems, and team leadership.

VEX

VEX Robotics Competition (VRC) is the world's largest and fastest-growing competitive robotics program for middle and high school students. The competition brings together teams from across the globe to design, build, and program robots that compete in an exciting game-based engineering challenge. Each season presents a new game with unique objectives, requiring teams to think creatively, work collaboratively, and continuously refine their designs.

The VEX V5 system provides teams with powerful, consistent materials including motors, sensors, and structural components, leveling the playing field and allowing innovation and engineering excellence to shine. Competitions emphasize not just technical performance, but also teamwork, sportsmanship, and the ability to think strategically under pressure. With divisions at local, regional, and world championship levels, VEX Robotics offers students a pathway to develop valuable STEM skills while participating in an engaging, competitive environment.

Nothing But Net - Team 9698B (Middle School)

In this season, the game objective is to shoot balls into high-mounted baskets to score points.

I co-founded and captained Team 9698B, guiding four classmates from initial concept to a fully operational robot. We managed the entire process‚Äîbrainstorming, CAD design, programming, and integration‚Äîas a collaborative team.

Our Creation & Key Innovations

  • Agile Movement: We built a holonomic drivetrain for superior mobility, allowing our robot to strafe and reposition with ease.
  • Efficient Collection & Launching: I designed a wide roller intake system to gather balls, which were then fed into a dual-flywheel launcher. By implementing a PID control loop with encoder feedback, we achieved consistent, high-accuracy shots.
  • Smart Architecture: Our "front-intake, front-shooter" design was not only intuitive for drivers but also enabled unique scoring angles, even from directly beneath the goal.
  • Autonomous Precision: I programmed a reliable 15-second autonomous routine that scored pre-loaded balls and navigated to a strategic starting position.

Tower Takeover - Team 9666A (High School)

In this season, the game objective is to place cubes into scoring zones and towers to achieve the highest score.

Entering high school, I founded a new robotics club and led Team 9666A. This time, I took on a mentorship role, guiding two beginners through their first build. We used project management tools like Notion to structure our workflow, with weekly goals and learning modules to ensure steady progress.

Our Robot & Key Features

  • High-Capacity Handling: We engineered a tilting tray mechanism that could collect and hold up to 10 cubes, using reversible rollers for precise control.
  • Articulated Stacking Arm: I developed a parallelogram lift with an articulating intake. This clever mechanism ensured a clean release of stacked cubes as the robot moved away.
  • Driver-Friendly Automation: To simplify the complex stacking task, I coded a 'one-touch stacking' macro. This driver-assist feature automated the lift-and-place sequence, allowing us to execute consistent, high-scoring stacks under pressure.

RoboMaster

RoboMaster is DJI's premier robotics competition that combines engineering innovation with strategic gameplay. University teams from around the world design, build, and program autonomous and manually-controlled robots to compete in an intense 5v5 battle format on a futuristic arena. The competition challenges teams across multiple disciplines: mechanical engineering for robust chassis and firing systems, electrical engineering for power management and sensors, computer vision for autonomous targeting, and software engineering for control algorithms and strategic coordination.

The RoboMaster University League (RMUL) serves as a premier platform for university students to showcase their technical skills, fostering innovation in robotics and providing real-world engineering experience. With over 200 participating teams worldwide, the competition pushes the boundaries of what's possible in tactical robotics and collaborative autonomous systems.

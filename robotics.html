Robotics Competition

My VEX Robotics Journey

My passion for robotics ignited in middle school through VEX Robotics Competitions. These events are more than just contests; they're a hands-on fusion of sport, strategy, and engineering, where students worldwide design, build, and program robots to solve a new game challenge each season. It was here I learned to lead, innovate, and turn ideas into a functioning, competitive machine.

About Team 9698B (Middle School)

2015-2016 Season: Nothing But Net

In this season, the game objective is to shoot balls into high-mounted baskets to score points.

I co-founded and captained Team 9698B, guiding four classmates from initial concept to a fully operational robot. We managed the entire process‚Äîbrainstorming, CAD design, programming, and integration‚Äîas a collaborative team.

Our Creation & Key Innovations

  • Agile Movement: We built a holonomic drivetrain for superior mobility, allowing our robot to strafe and reposition with ease.
  • Efficient Collection & Launching: I designed a wide roller intake system to gather balls, which were then fed into a dual-flywheel launcher. By implementing a PID control loop with encoder feedback, we achieved consistent, high-accuracy shots.
  • Smart Architecture: Our "front-intake, front-shooter" design was not only intuitive for drivers but also enabled unique scoring angles, even from directly beneath the goal.
  • Autonomous Precision: I programmed a reliable 15-second autonomous routine that scored pre-loaded balls and navigated to a strategic starting position.

About Team 9666A (High School)

2019-2022 Season: Tower Takeover

In this season, the game objective is to place cubes into scoring zones and towers to achieve the highest score.

Entering high school, I founded a new robotics club and led Team 9666A. This time, I took on a mentorship role, guiding two beginners through their first build. We used project management tools like Notion to structure our workflow, with weekly goals and learning modules to ensure steady progress.

Our Robot & Key Features

  • High-Capacity Handling: We engineered a tilting tray mechanism that could collect and hold up to 10 cubes, using reversible rollers for precise control.
  • Articulated Stacking Arm: I developed a parallelogram lift with an articulating intake. This clever mechanism ensured a clean release of stacked cubes as the robot moved away.
  • Driver-Friendly Automation: To simplify the complex stacking task, I coded a 'one-touch stacking' macro. This driver-assist feature automated the lift-and-place sequence, allowing us to execute consistent, high-scoring stacks under pressure.
